Journey to the end of England
Ready to start our LEJOG journey
9 August 2021 (published 26 November 2022)
7 min read

While cycling from Lands End to John o'Groats was a trip of a lifetime, I also fundraised for Mind UK along the way. My fundraising page has closed now, but if you would like to donate to this fantastic charity, donations are accepted via their website.

Months of preparation for LEJOG and the big day had finally arrived! We were off to Lands End to begin our 1,000 mile journey across the length of the UK.

Streatham to Paddington

We'd decided to get the train to Penzance then cycle to Lands End, where we’d be spending the night before the Grand Départ the next morning. Our first test was the cycle to Paddington station with our bikes loaded up with all of our things for the next 16 days. Of course, it was pouring down with rain for the first cycle of the trip, thankfully not a sign of things to come. First hurdle for me… actually getting on my bike. The weight and width of the loaded panniers made things a little unstable, and my bike nearly ended up flat on the floor while I was trying to get on it. Second attempt and I was safely on.

It’s a fairly straight-forward cycle to Paddington for us, though this was both of our first cycles with fully-laden bikes. We’re both quite used to cycling with the London traffic, Jonathan more so and much more confident with it than I am. A few km in and I was thinking that this actually wasn’t all that bad (my cycles often turn out like this…), and the nerves slowly started to turn into excitement for the days ahead.

One wrong turn and just under an hour later, we were safely at Paddington, with plenty of time to stock up on snacks (the carb-loading starts here!) before the 5 hour journey to Penzance.

We survived the very first cycle!

End of the line

Boarding started late and the train was fully booked, so it felt a little hectic trying to unload our bikes and get them racked in time. Our seats were at the other end of the train to our allocated bike spots so I held down the fort on the platform minding our bags while Jonathan ran back and forth with both of the bikes.

Almost time to go...

Just over three hours later, we were crossing the River Tamar into Cornwall, over Brunel's famous Royal Albert Bridge. Strange to think that in less than 48 hours, we'd be crossing the river in the other direction, but by ferry rather than train.

The not-as-famous Tamar Bridge

Once we were in Cornwall, the train started to thin out and we were soon in Penzance, the end of the line!

All you need for 16 days of camping and cycling

Loaded up and ready to go again

Penzance

This was both of our first times in Penzance, but we weren’t sticking around for too long as we were keen to get to Lands End to set up camp. Just outside the station, a sign welcomes you to Penzance in the Cornish language.

Pensans a'gas dynergh

We wanted to try out Willy Wallers ice cream, just a short walk from the train station. We saw a few groups of cyclists cycle past with Prostate Cancer UK jerseys on heading towards Lands End for the end of their JOGLE. Now very close to the point of no return and faced with having to cycle on unfamiliar roads, my nerves had well and truly returned, but seeing people about to finish their End-to-End journey and having a great time doing so gave me a little bit of confidence. My sorbet from Willy Wallers was also a very welcome distraction!

Sorbet and palm trees

Penzance to Lands End

The Cicerone recommended route between Penzance and Lands End is the A30, which we would be taking the next day on our return to Penzance, but I’d planned an alternative, more scenic coastal route to take us to our campsite while we had the time. All going well, I was hoping we could cycle down to a beach and spend a couple of hours there.

However, I’d made one catastrophic mistake… I hadn’t checked the elevation profile. As soon as we’d left Penzance, it was straight into a 14% incline. No easing ourselves into it or getting used to the added weight on our bikes. It was a bit of a shock to the system, and I ended up walking most of this first incline, which sparked a few laughs from a passing postman - they must see this all of the time!

The downhills weren’t much better. I wasn’t yet used to the speed the added weight gives you on a decline and I found myself hanging on for dear life. We were also on a 60mph country lane, a far cry from the gridlock London traffic I’m used to, so we had no choice but to persevere with the hills. At least the scenery was good - green Cornish countryside either side of us and the sea fast-approaching in the distance.

Getting closer...

We gave up on the beach trip pretty easily. We were already pretty high above sea level and there was no way we were subjecting ourselves to more hills than we needed to! We’d taken more time to get to Lands End than we thought we would, but we turned one last corner and our campsite was finally in sight.

Lands End

We were staying at the recently-opened Lands End Camping & Glamping, less than a mile away from the famous Lands End landmark. We very clearly looked like we were about to start LEJOG as the owners were laughing at our decision to bring an electric hook-up, and bet that we would end up ditching it within a few days. They even offered to buy it from us for £15, but we were determined to get some use out of it.

Tent pitched up, electric hook-up untangled and having worked up some appetite on the surprise hills on our cycle to Lands End, we walked the short distance into Sennen to grab some food. We walked past the first/last inn in England - can't wait to see the first/last inn in Scotland at the other end!

Our new home

First & Last Inn in England

There weren’t too many options for food in Sennen, so, being by the seaside, it had to be a chippy. I think everyone else in Lands End had the same idea, as we had quite a long wait outside for our food. The perfect opportunity to pick up some local Tribute ale.

Tribute and chips

We walked down to the Lands End landmark to check things out. I'd only read bad things about Lands End, that it’s purely a tourist trap (true) and that there isn't much to see or do (also true). We’d really lucked out with the beautiful sunset, which only added to the stunning views from the most south-westernly tip of mainland England.

Morph!

It was the perfect time for photos with the famous signpost, as most of the crowds had died down and the peddlers charging £15 for a photo had already left for the day. We also saw someone who had just finished their JOGLE cycle!

We walked along the coastline to stretch our legs, hoping to make it to Sennen Cove or at least catch a glimpse of the RMS Mulheim shipwreck. Unfortunately we didn't get as far as the shipwreck as it was getting dark quite quickly, but the walk along the coastline was a lovely end to a long day regardless.

Back to the campsite for a timed 5 minute token shower and an early night ahead of the big day tomorrow. I hadn’t seen such a clear sky in a very long time!

So many stars!
